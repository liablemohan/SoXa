📜 Doctoral Thesis

Kāvya Aesthetics in Late Classical Sanskrit Poetry: A Re-evaluation of Rasa Theory

रस-सिद्धान्तस्य पुनर्मूल्यांकनम् — कालिदासोत्तर-संस्कृत-काव्ये

Dr. Meera Krishnamurthy · Banaras Hindu University · 2024 · Ph.D. Dissertation · ⏱ ~22 min read
Kāvya Aesthetics Rasa Theory Abhinavagupta Open Access

The theory of rasa — aesthetic emotional experience — stands as one of the most sophisticated contributions of classical Sanskrit thought to global aesthetics. From its origins in the नाट्यशास्त्र (Nāṭyaśāstra) of Bharata to its radical reformulation by Abhinavagupta in the अभिनवभारती (Abhinavabhāratī), rasa theory underwent transformations that the present thesis argues have been systematically obscured by post-colonial scholarship in its privileging of Western aesthetic categories.[1]

This dissertation offers the first comprehensive comparative analysis of rasa theory that accounts for all major stages of the tradition: Bharata's eightfold schema, Udbhaṭa's additions, Ānandavardhana's dhvani revolution, and Abhinavagupta's phenomenological synthesis. The central argument is that rasa, properly understood, is not a theory of emotion but a theory of consciousness — the चमत्कार (camatkāra) that attends aesthetic experience is structurally identical to the recognition (प्रत्यभिज्ञा, pratyabhijñā) described in Kashmir Śaivism.[2]

Background and Scope

The rasa sūtra of Bharata — विभावानुभावव्यभिचारिसंयोगाद्रसनिष्पत्तिः (vibhāvānubhāvavyabhicārisaṃyogādrasaniṣpattiḥ) — has generated more scholarly commentary than perhaps any other sentence in the history of Sanskrit literary theory. Its deceptively simple formulation — that aesthetic emotion arises from the conjunction of determinants, consequents, and transient states — conceals a profound epistemological problem: how can an emotion that is not directly experienced be enjoyed?

"Rasa is not the emotion depicted in the poem. It is the emotion recognised in oneself through the medium of the poem — and that recognition is what Abhinavagupta calls sādhāraṇīkaraṇa, universalisation."

— Dr. Meera Krishnamurthy, Chapter III, p. 147

The problem of the enjoyment of negative emotions — grief, fear, disgust — in a context of detached aesthetic appreciation is what Abhinavagupta resolved through his concept of सामाजिक (sāmājika) — the ideal connoisseur who, through cultivation of aesthetic sensitivity, can experience the transformative recognition that aesthetic emotion offers.

Methodology

The dissertation employs a tripartite methodology: first, close philological reading of primary Sanskrit texts in their original; second, engagement with modern phenomenological aesthetics (Ingarden, Gadamer, Merleau-Ponty) as comparative interlocutors rather than superior frameworks; third, reception-history analysis of how rasa theory has been taught and transmitted in contemporary Sanskrit academies in Varanasi and Mysore.[3]

Primary Sources

The primary texts consulted include the Nāṭyaśāstra (ed. Ghosh), Dhvanyāloka (ed. Krishnamoorthy), Abhinavabhāratī (ed. Ramakrishna Kavi), Locana (ed. Gnoli), and Tantrāloka chapters on śaktipāta for the philosophical context of Abhinavagupta's aesthetics. All translations are the author's own.

Field Research

Twelve months of field research were conducted in Varanasi between 2021–2022, including structured interviews with fifteen scholars trained in the traditional व्याकरण and अलङ्कार curricula, as well as participant observation in advanced śāstra seminars at Sampurnanand Sanskrit University.

Key Findings

The thesis establishes four principal findings that advance the existing scholarship:

Finding 1 — The Śaiva substrate of Abhinava's rasa theory: Abhinavagupta's reconceptualisation of rasa as a form of brahma-sāmānya (universalised consciousness-bliss) is not a late addition to aesthetic theory but the culmination of a continuous tradition stretching from Bharata's own Vedic context.

Finding 2 — The nine-rasa problem: The addition of śānta (tranquillity) as the ninth rasa, contested since the 9th century, is defended here not merely on canonical grounds but through a new argument derived from the structure of Abhinavagupta's phenomenology of aesthetic recognition.

Finding 3 — Dhvani and rasa as unified: Ānandavardhana's dhvani theory and rasa theory, treated as separate developments in standard scholarship, are shown to be two aspects of a single theory of aesthetic resonance — dhvani being the linguistic mechanism, rasa the experiential outcome.

Finding 4 — Living tradition: The traditional Varanasi scholars interviewed for this thesis maintain rasa theory as a living practice of textual interpretation, not merely a historical curiosity, demonstrating the continued relevance of the Sanskrit aesthetic tradition.

Conclusions

This thesis has argued that rasa theory, in its fully developed form as articulated by Abhinavagupta, constitutes a rigorous phenomenology of aesthetic consciousness that not only rivals but in certain respects surpasses Western aesthetic theories in its sophistication. The Śaiva context of Abhinavagupta's formulation is not a metaphysical excess to be bracketed away, but the key to understanding why rasa alone, among world aesthetic theories, explains the paradox of the enjoyment of negative emotions with such philosophical precision.

Future research directions include: a computational corpus study of rasa-related terminology across the GRETIL Sanskrit corpus; a pedagogical study of how rasa is taught in contemporary Indian university Sanskrit departments; and a comparative study with Chinese aesthetic theory, where parallel concerns about emotional distance in art have generated related conceptual solutions.
